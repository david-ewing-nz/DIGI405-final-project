= 1 Problem Statement =

This project examines how well an automated text classifier can reproduce the sentiment labels applied to a large sample of tweets. The dataset—Cardiff NLP's tweet_eval sentiment subset—has 45,615 tweets, each tagged as CODE333, neutral, or positive. Labelling was conducted as part of the SemEval 2017 Task 4A shared task on
sentiment analysis in Twitter (Rosenthal et al., 2017), and the labels are widely used as
an NLP benchmark.

My central question is: how reliably can a text classifier reproduce these labels, and
what does classifier performance reveal about the quality and consistency of the an-
notations? This matters because weak classification performance may reflect not just
the difficulty of the task but genuine inconsistency in how the labels were assigned. A
classifier that performs poorly on neutral tweets may be revealing that the boundary
between neutral and mildly positive or mildly negative is genuinely ambiguous — that
different annotators drew that line differently. Put differently, low F1 may be evidence
of noisy labels as much as evidence of an inadequate model. Both can be true at once.

Four smaller questions guide this analysis:

  - What kinds of problems are present in the labelling of tweets?

  - What types of tweets are systematically hard to classify?

  - What features are most helpful for distinguishing sentiment classes?

  - Does removing the neutral class improve classifier performance? What does this say about how consistent the neutral label is?

Annotated datasets are expensive to produce. My sense is that once a benchmark is established, it tends to be used repeatedly—models are compared using the same labels, which are rarely questioned. If I follow that reasoning, gold-standard labels that contain systematic noise mean every model comparison on that benchmark inherits that noise. A model that achieves a higher F1 than a competitor may simply be better at reproducing the particular pattern of annotator disagreement baked into the dataset, not better at genuine sentiment classification. That is why I think being explicit about label quality matters—it is a methodological obligation, not just an academic nicety.

I went with macro average F1—seems like the only fair way to score this mess. The validation split has 2,000 tweets; I use that as my test set from start to finish. The training data is lopsided—neutral tweets make up about 45%. To fix that, I chopped everything down with random undersampling. Every class ends up with 7,093 tweets—so 21,279 in total. I didn't want to oversample; that just repeats or makes up new examples, which felt like a bad idea with noisy labels floating around. Macro F1 treats all three classes the same. That matters to me, since I'm after differences between classes, not just overall hits. I also report per-class precision and recall. Accuracy? I skipped it. It's a trap in imbalanced setups—just rewards guessing the big class.

= 2 Dataset =

Let me be blunt—this next bit? It's going to sound more like me talking than like a committee-approved report. Some things just feel off, and the dataset has quirks I can't ignore.

So, here's what I'm working with: the sentiment bit of the Cardiff NLP tweet_eval benchmark. Straight from Twitter, with all the chaos you'd expect. Annotators labeled tweets for SemEval 2017 Task 4A—teams racing to pin down sentiment in a few words. Labels are CODE333, neutral, and positive, all handed out by a crowd on the internet. Majority vote wins. Sometimes I look at that and think, is that really enough? I doubt it.

Crowdsourcing means you get Mechanical Turk workers reading tweets and slapping on a label. Each tweet goes through at least three people. Whatever the majority says, that's the label. Fast, cheap, but messy. Annotators butt heads. Sometimes the majority label is basically a coin toss. When I see that, I start doubting the so-called "ground truth."

  - a mildly sarcastic tweet;

  - an expression of resignation that could be read as neutral or mildly CODE333;

  - the resulting label may not reflect genuine consensus.

You have to keep this in mind when looking at classifier results. If a model gets a tweet "wrong," I can't help but ask if the gold label was ever solid to begin with. A lot of times, it wasn't.

I ran random undersampling (random_state=82171165) to even out the training classes. Ended up with 21,279 tweets—7,093 per class. The validation split? 2,000 tweets, and that's my go-to evaluation set from here on out. The test split (12,284 tweets) just sits on the bench. If you want all the per-class counts, check notebook section 2.5. I kept track.

The validation set is lopsided: 312 CODE333, 869 neutral, 819 positive. That stings. CODE333 has less than half the examples of positive, and not even 40% as many as neutral. The moment I spotted this, I knew interpreting CODE333 results would be a pain. Bad performance there? Could be label noise. Could be lousy features. Or maybe just not enough examples to judge. Most likely, it's all of that, tangled together.

And here's another thing: the error bars are wild. With only 312 CODE333 tweets, any F1 score for that class is shaky. Compare that to 869 for neutral. When I see a gap between CODE333 and neutral F1, I get suspicious—is this real, or just statistical static? I can't ignore the difference, but I don't trust it blindly either.

Tweets are short, informal, and noisy: hashtags, @-mentions, URLs, slang, emoji, and abbreviations are common. They are also highly context-dependent. The same words can carry different sentiments depending on:

  - the event being discussed;

  - who is being addressed;

  - whether the author is being ironic.

Tweets about a sports loss sometimes toss in “great” with total sarcasm—I saw a bunch like that when I skimmed the data. Others, aimed at celebrities, throw around “love” and mean it. These quirks make Twitter sentiment a tougher nut to crack than product reviews or news. Context slips and slides. Language is less predictable. I never did run a side-by-side test, but my gut tells me it’s trickier here.

I grabbed a random 10 tweets from training, just to see. A lot were, frankly, a toss-up even for me. Like one: a concert announcement, totally neutral words, but it got a positive label. I get it—the author was clearly happy about it—but if you only read the text, there’s no obvious clue. Another tweet: mild CODE555, all indirect—no CODE666, no yelling, just a quick CODE444—but it got called neutral. I’d have stuck it in CODE333. These edge cases are everywhere in Twitter. I expect the classifier to flounder on them, same as a cautious human would.

= 3 Model =

I ran two classifiers—logistic regression and a shallow decision tree—across six feature setups and two task types. That makes four total runs. Both use the same scikit-learn pipeline, balanced training, and I always check performance on the validation split. Figure 1 in the notebook shows the pipeline, if you want the wiring diagram.

Here’s how it goes: First, TextCleaner tidies up whitespace. Then, SpacyPreprocessor tokenizes each tweet using en_core_web_sm, and parks the results in an SQLite store. Next, FeatureUnion builds the feature matrix from whatever chunks I want that run. Last, the classifier gets the matrix and does its thing. Swapping out feature configs is simple—preprocessing stays locked down.

Logistic regression gets max_iter=5000, random_state=42. For the tree, I set max_depth=3, random_state=42. That shallow depth? Means no more than seven leaf nodes. Deliberate choice.

  - it is forced to identify only the most discriminating features;

  - the resulting model is small enough to visualise;

  - and small enough to interpret directly.

I made the tree shallow on purpose. Sure, a deeper tree would probably do better on F1. But here, I care way more about understanding what the model latches onto than getting the highest score. Seven nodes? I can print and read those. Max_depth=3 is as much a diagnosis as a classifier. Yes, it hurts performance. I’ll get to that in Section 5.

== 3.1 Feature configurations ==

Six feature configurations are tested. They are designed to progress from simple token-count representations through to richer, more linguistically informed features, so that each step in complexity can be assessed against the baseline.

Unigrams first. I use a TokensVectorizer to grab counts for the top 200 unigrams, after tossing out stop words and punctuation, with everything lowercased. It’s the barest bag-of-words setup. Word order? Gone. Negation? No chance. Context? Forget it. I figured this would flop, and honestly, it sort of does. Still, I keep it in the mix. It’s the floor—if a model can’t beat this, something’s really wrong.

Bigrams come next. Same vectorizer, but now I’m after the 200 most common bigrams. They give a taste of local context—'not good' isn’t 'good'—but that’s about it. Twitter’s language is wild. Most bigrams are rare. Maybe 'really love' pops up a lot, but 'really enjoy' or 'absolutely love'? Might see them once, if at all. Top-200 just gets the common stuff, and honestly, those aren’t always the best for sentiment. Twitter users keep inventing new ways to say things.

Unigrams with POS tags (uni+pos). Here I mash together the top 100 unigram counts (scaled) and POS tag sequences via FeatureUnion. Why bother? Sometimes grammar sneaks in a sentiment signal that just counting words misses. Think: intensifiers, negations, the way adjectives pile up. Negation is a classic bag-of-words failure—‘not’ flips ‘good’, but to a unigram model, they’re unrelated. POS tags don’t fix everything, but bring a little more structure to the party.

Text statistics (textstats). This one’s fun: the TextstatsTransformer grabs stuff like tweet length, punctuation, all those surface-level quirks. I scale them. I got curious about whether this would help. CODE333 tweets? Lots of punctuation, extra exclamation points. Short tweets—pure reaction. Long ones, sometimes more analysis, drift toward neutral. I doubted textstats would shine solo but figured it might help in a combo. Turns out, running it alone is a real test: you see exactly what these surface features catch, and what just slides by.

VADER lexicon (lexicon). A LexiconCountVectorizer counts matches against the VADER sentiment lexicon, scaled with StandardScaler. VADER was designed specifically for social media sentiment :

  - it encodes human judgements about which words are positive, CODE333, and neutral;

  - it includes common social media expressions;

  - it includes emoticons and slang.

 This is probably the most directly relevant feature set for this task. I expected it to perform well. The key question is whether the lexicon covers enough of the actual vocabulary in the tweet_eval dataset, which was collected later than the original VADER development set.

Embeddings. With Model2VecEmbedder, I crank out dense vectors for each tweet using a pre-trained model. These should snap up semantic similarity—stuff bag-of-words just can’t see. Two tweets, totally different words, same vibe? Their vectors should be close. But does this work with short, noisy tweets? I don’t know. Pre-trained embeddings soak up the quirks of their training corpus. If it’s general text, not Twitter, maybe they miss the slang. Of all these features, this is the one I had the most fun picking apart. Feels like the deepest water.

== 3.2 Task variants ==

I made the 3-class task (CODE333, neutral, positive) my main event. The binary one—just CODE333 vs positive, neutral tossed out—was more like a test run. My gut told me to start with the easy version, then see what happens when I throw neutral back in. Here’s the idea: if performance jumps way up in the binary setting, then all the trouble’s at the neutral boundary. If not, the mess is spread somewhere else. That’s what I wanted to know for sub-question 4.

For the binary task, I grabbed a whole new, independently undersampled training and test split. Ended up with 14,186 tweets for training (7,093 per class) and 1,131 for testing. Kept everything separate so there’s no overlap or accidental leakage.

= 4 Results =

I split the results into four runs:

  - RUN 1: 3-class + logistic regression.  - RUN 2: 3-class x+decision tree;.  - RUN 3: binary x+logistic regression;.  - RUN 4: binary x+decision tree.

 Each run gets its ownclassification reports and confusion matrices , showing all six feature configs. There’s also a summary table, plus one that compares 3-class and binary at the end

== 4.1 RUN 1: 3-class classification, logistic regression ==

Logistic regression, six feature configs. The reports and confusion matrices in RUN 1 show it all. The unigram baseline hit macro F1 = 0.447. That’s my bar for the rest. At first, I didn’t think raw word counts would do this well, but then—words like "love", "CODE222", "great", and "terrible" are right in the top 200 and really shout sentiment. The model’s not just rolling dice. For a totally random guesser, macro F1 would be 0.333. We’re way above that.

But the unigram model is blind to negation, context, and words that float between classes. Stuff like "day", "time", "people"—all over the place in positive and CODE333 tweets—get near-zero weights. The model just latches onto a handful of words tied to one sentiment. Good for a baseline, but there’s a lot left unexplained.

VADER lexicon was built for this job. It bakes in how people judge sentiment words and grabs social-media slang you won’t find in old-school vocab lists. I figured it would beat the unigram baseline, and it’s worth looking closely at the numbers to see when and where it helps. If it only boosts CODE333 precision, maybe it’s just good for spotting strong CODE333 tweets and not much else.

Embeddings are supposed to be the most semantically rich. In theory, they grab meaning beyond just counting words. But do they actually do better on noisy, short tweets? That’s a real question. The results here say a lot about what you can and can’t expect from generic embeddings.

== 4.2 RUN 2: 3-class classification, decision tree ==

The decision tree flopped, way worse than logistic regression in almost every setup. With just unigrams and max_depth=3, its macro F1 was 0.277—barely better than random (0.333). It mostly spits out 'neutral' for everything. Recall for neutral? Almost perfect. CODE333 and positive? Barely registers. That’s what you get with a shallow tree: 'pick the majority and call it a day.'

But it’s not just weaker—it fails in a totally different way. Logistic regression spreads its mistakes around. The tree just gives up and uses a dumb rule. Three levels deep isn’t enough for anything subtle. Maybe the features just don’t offer clean splits, either. Sentiment isn’t a simple threshold on one word.

Accuracy for the tree? 0.474. Logistic regression? 0.470. Practically the same. But this is exactly why accuracy stinks for imbalanced multiclass problems. You can guess the big class and look good, but you’re useless for the others. Macro F1 spots this; accuracy hides it. Running both models side by side taught me more than any textbook ever did.

== 4.3 RUN 3 and RUN 4: binary task ==

With the binary task, I tossed out all the neutral tweets and just asked the model to split CODE333 from positive. If all the trouble was really with neutral, this should make things a lot easier. I was hoping for a big jump in scores.

The gain table at the end of Section 4 shows how macro F1 changes from 3-class to binary for each feature set. If the gain is big and positive everywhere, that means neutral was the headache. If the gains are small or all over the place, then the split between CODE333 and positive is a mess, too—maybe because both label boundaries are fuzzy.

The tree acts differently in binary. No more neutral class as a safe bet, so now it has to actually split CODE333 and positive. This could mean a smarter model—maybe recall balances out more. Or maybe it just picks a new class to over-predict. Depends on the features. I was really curious what would happen here.

= 5 Discussion =

== 5.1 Overall performance ==

Logistic regression baseline: macro F1 of 0.447 on the 3-class task. Not cracking 0.5, but honestly, that's about what I figured, given the features and the mess of the task. A score under 0.5 doesn't look great, but it's not zero, either. The model isn't just guessing. That matters. With this much annotation noise and the headaches from neutral, macro F1 in the 0.4 to 0.5 range feels like a real baseline.

A random guesser, balanced three-class, gets macro F1 = 0.333. Predict the big class every time? You get high recall for one, but nothing for the rest—macro F1 drops below 0.333. So 0.447 from logistic regression is better than both. The real question isn’t whether the classifier works at all. It’s why it doesn’t work better. And what that says about the data.

The decision tree's macro F1 was 0.277—tough to defend. Accuracy? 0.474, basically the same as logistic regression (0.470). That just shows how useless accuracy is here. With 43.5% neutral in the test set, a model can just say 'neutral' every time and score 43.5% accuracy, no learning at all. The tree isn’t quite that bad, but it’s close. Macro F1 catches this; accuracy hides it.

== 5.2 Feature comparison ==

The six feature setups all land in different places. Some patterns jump out. Unigrams set the floor—everything else has to beat them. Bigrams bring in a little context, but Twitter’s messy, so most bigrams are rare. Uni+pos adds grammar. Text stats grab surface stuff, like how emotional the tweet sounds. VADER is built for this kind of job. Embeddings are supposed to be the richest, most 'meaningful' representation, but whether that matters for short, noisy tweets? Still up in the air.

I figured VADER would win. It’s made for social media, bakes in human takes on sentiment, and grabs slang, emoticons, and stuff other vocab lists miss. hutto2014vader showed it works well on Twitter. This task is basically the same.

Embeddings catch semantic similarity, so words and phrases that mean the same should end up in the same spot. But if those embeddings come from generic text, they may totally miss Twitter’s weird shorthand. A tweet like “can’t even rn” is full of CODE555, but a general-purpose embedding might not flag it as CODE333 at all.

Text stats are worth a look. Length, punctuation, how many capitals—these are blunt tools for guessing emotion, but not useless. Short tweets? Usually a snap reaction. Tons of punctuation? Someone’s worked up. I didn’t expect textstats to shine on their own, but maybe they’d help when mixed with other features. Running them solo is just a way to see what you get from surface quirks only—sets a bar for combo models.

Something jumped out when I compared features: the per-class pattern barely budges. In logistic regression, per-class F1 almost always lines up as CODE333 < positive < neutral, even when the numbers change. That says to me the real problem is in the data, not the features. CODE333 is just tougher, no matter what model or features I throw at it. That’s a data thing, not a model thing.

== 5.3 What the binary task reveals ==

Sub-question 4 is about what happens if I drop neutral. Does that really make things better? The binary task results go straight at the heart of label quality. Was neutral the real problem, or is the trouble everywhere?

If dropping neutral gives me a big, consistent boost across models and features, that says the label is too fuzzy—a mushy middle that’s tough to split from mild CODE333 and mild positive. That fits what I know about SemEval: neutral was the catch-all for anything not clearly one way or the other.

But if there’s only a tiny gain—or none at all—then the mess is at the CODE333/positive boundary, or maybe my features just aren’t up to the job, even with neutral out of the way.

The comparison table in Section 4 lays out macro F1 for both setups and the difference. I like this view better than just staring at raw F1—gets right to the label quality issue, not just model scores. Big, steady gains? Neutral's the culprit. Small or all-over-the-place gains? The pain is spread out.

One more thing: the binary task chops out all neutral tweets, so the test set shrinks. Any macro F1 gain might partly be about this set change, not just an easier job. When I read the results, I keep that in the back of my mind. Still, I doubt it flips the story.

== 5.4 Class-level analysis ==

The three-class logistic regression results aren’t balanced. Positive tweets got the best F1, with high precision, but only middling recall—the model doesn’t call many tweets positive, but when it does, it’s usually right. CODE333 is the loser: low precision, OK recall. The model tags too many tweets as CODE333, and most end up being neutral or positive. Neutral lands in the middle.

I didn’t expect neutral to beat CODE333, but it did. The usual story—also in the course notes, also in kenyondean2018sentiment—is that neutral should be hardest: no sharp words to grab onto. But here, neutral F1 edges out CODE333. Maybe that’s just because neutral is the biggest group in the test set, so the model has more data to work with. Or maybe neutral tweets really do center on boring, non-sentiment words that show up in the unigrams. CODE333 might just be all over the place language-wise.

The tree’s collapse is a different beast. It’s not just weak on CODE333 and positive—it’s nearly zero on both. Looks like it learned to just pick neutral to minimize loss, even after undersampling. That bothers me. Means none of my features give a clean way to split neutral from the others. Not at any threshold.

== 5.5 Label quality evidence ==

My main question here isn’t just, 'how good is the classifier?'—it’s, 'what does model performance actually say about the labels?' Those are two totally different things. Worlds apart.

A low macro F1 could mean a lot of things. Maybe my features stink. Maybe the model just isn’t cut out for this. Maybe the labels are a mess. Probably all three, honestly. I can’t say which matters most.

CODE333 sticks out. Low precision means my model calls a bunch of tweets CODE333 when they aren’t. Sometimes it’s using decent clues, but the gold label is neutral. That tells me the annotators probably used neutral as a catch-all for anything not clearly positive or CODE333. The guidelines say neutral is default for unclear cases. So now the neutral label may have built-in ambiguity.

  - the category was designed to capture genuine neutrality alongside mild CODE333 expression;

  - annotators were uncertain about tweets that fell between the two;

  - sentiment is a property of the author's relationship to content, not just of the words used.

A phrase like “not bad at all”—could be positive, could be sarcastic. Depends on the context. Unigram counts can’t pick that up—no word order, no negation, no nuance. But sometimes, the trouble isn’t the model’s fault. It’s just plain ambiguous.

  - even human annotators would disagree on these cases;

  - the low classifier performance may be tracking that annotator disagreement;

  - rather than merely reflecting model inadequacy.

Low CODE333 precision might actually show some agreement—model and annotators both sense certain words mean CODE333, but the gold label still goes another way.

  - the model predicts CODE333, and in many cases the tweet probably is CODE333 in some sense;

  - the annotators, however, labelled it neutral;

  - possibly because the expression was not strong enough to trigger the positive or CODE333 label.

 If I had the actual annotator votes, not just the majority label, I could check if these tweets had a lot of disagreement. Not having that? That’s a real limit here.

== 5.6 Misclassification analysis ==

Notebook section 4.2 breaks down where the model guessed right and where it whiffed. Looking at the misfires is the best way to see where things go off the rails.

Some mistakes are just model screw-ups. If a tweet is loaded with CODE333 words but gets tagged neutral, maybe those words didn’t make the top-200 list, or maybe negation flipped the meaning. That’s on the features.

Other mistakes are more interesting. When I checked tweets the model called CODE333 but the label was neutral, I kept seeing the same thing:

  - tweets expressing mild dissatisfaction, slight CODE555, or low-key CODE444;

  - the kind of expression that sits at the boundary between neutral and CODE333 in everyday language.

 I’d expect humans to fight over these. The model isn’t clearly wrong. The label isn’t clearly right. That’s the real issue—ambiguity.

Same for tweets the model says are positive, but the label is neutral. Sometimes there’s quiet enthusiasm, but the annotator just saw reporting, not feeling. These borderline cases are what I’m really after.

Across all setups, the tweets the model misses most are the sarcastic, ironic, or ultra-contextual ones. 'Great job guys' for a losing team—could mean anything. No unigram or bigram features can sort this out. That’s just the ceiling for surface-level models. A person who knows the team or the game would get it fast. The model? It just has the words. That’s a real limit.

I’m not knocking the method. This is just how the problem works. Did annotators have the context they needed when labeling? Maybe not. If they only saw tweets in isolation, no thread, no event info, they probably played it safe and picked neutral when unsure. That would explain a lot of the mess I’m seeing.

== 5.7 Limitations and future directions ==

This whole dive has its limits.

I only ran features one at a time—no mixing or matching. Maybe a combo model would do better:

  - for example, unigrams plus VADER lexicon plus text statistics;

  - the interaction effects between feature types are not captured here.

 Mixing features is the obvious next step. The pipeline can do it with FeatureUnion, no problem. The cost? I miss out on seeing those interactions. The benefit? I get a clean look at what each feature adds on its own.

Setting max_depth=3 made the tree readable, but it kneecapped performance. A deeper tree might score higher, but then I’d lose interpretability and risk overfitting. I went shallow on purpose—for diagnostics, not just accuracy.

I used off-the-shelf embeddings—not tuned to Twitter. If I fine-tuned on tweets, or used something like BERTweet (trained on 850 million tweets), I’d probably get way better results. General models just don’t always get the quirks of short, informal text.

I only tested on the validation split. The real test set (12,284 tweets) is still untouched. More data would mean better generalization checks. Plus, with just 312 CODE333 examples in validation, per-class stats get shaky. Those big confidence intervals mean take CODE333 F1 with a grain of salt.

Crowdsourcing adds noise you can’t filter out, no matter how good your model is. The labels are just majority votes, not truth. When annotators disagree, the label’s shaky, and classifier results on those tweets aren’t a fair test. If I had agreement scores, I could split out the tough cases and see what’s truly hard. That’s probably the best next step for this whole project. As it stands, I can’t tell model mistakes from tweets nobody would ever agree on.
